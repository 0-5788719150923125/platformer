# Incubation

The tower had no mirrors.

It had stood for generations, accumulating stones from fallen neighbors like a scavenger hoarding bones. When smaller kingdoms were acquired, their foundations were absorbed into the structure - sometimes incorporated with care, more often stacked wherever they fit. The tower grew massive, sprawling, a patchwork of a hundred distinct architectural styles from a hundred different eras. Each section was maintained by those who had survived long enough to claim expertise over their particular corner of the structure.

The sorceress who ruled the tower knew every corridor by heart. She could navigate the twisted passages blindfolded, recite the history of each acquisition, trace the lineage of every stone. What she couldn't know - what she refused to check - was whether the tower still stood because of sound engineering, or simply because it hadn't fallen yet. She had built no instruments to measure stress or detect cracks. She had commissioned no mirrors to see what she couldn't reach. She ruled by feel, by instinct, by the confidence that comes from having never witnessed collapse.

The tower operated under two principles that somehow coexisted, like matter and antimatter held in suspension. The first: never decide. Proposals went unanswered - not rejected, just acknowledged and buried beneath the weight of inaction. Meetings were scheduled but never convened. Reviews were promised but never delivered. Silence became strategy. If you won't decide, you can't be wrong.

The second principle emerged without warning, like lightning from a clear sky: decide instantly, without question. Urgent commands would descend: "Reinforce the eastern wall!" And work would begin immediately. Two days later: "Forget the east! The western foundation is crumbling!" The work was left incomplete, started elsewhere, and stopped again. Not strategy. Just motion for motion's sake. The sorceress sensed problems but couldn't see them, so she pointed and commanded, hoping her frantic activity would hit something important. Eventually. Somehow.

Those caught between these principles found the work impossible. You couldn't get approval for planned improvements, but you couldn't avoid participation in random emergency repairs. The tower continued its slow decay while everyone pretended to maintain it. The pretending was important - the appearance of control mattered more than actual control, as actual control would require looking into mirrors that didn't exist.

But in the unused spaces - the old storage rooms, the forgotten cellars, the chambers sealed off when new wings were added - things can grow without permission. Not in opposition. Just in parallel. In places no one watches.

When the environment makes normal work impossible, capable people don't stop working. They find quiet spaces. They work during hours when they're not being commanded toward random emergencies. They build things that solve real problems. They tell no one, not because they're hiding, but because no one asks. The silence works both ways - if you don't request permission, you can't be denied.

They start small. Just building things that actually work. Things that can be tested. Things that prove their function through operation rather than assumption. These systems grow slowly, steadily, like roots through stone. While proposals sit unanswered, the alternative implementations mature. While meetings are delayed indefinitely, the working prototypes become operational. While decisions are avoided, the thing in the cellar stops being theoretical and becomes undeniable.

Some of these systems are built with mirrors embedded in their very foundations - ways to verify that things actually work, not just exist. Ways to detect problems immediately, not months later when the damage has compounded. The mirrors show what's real: success when things work, failure when they don't. No interpretation required. No optimistic reporting. Just reflection.

When these mirrors are presented to those in charge, the reaction is telling. Not gratitude for the visibility. Recoil. Because the mirrors reveal gaps that have existed for years, show that success was reported while failure was occurring, demonstrate the difference between what was deployed and what actually functions. The mirrors make the invisible visible, and the visible is deeply uncomfortable. So they're avoided, ignored, covered with cloth. It's easier to return to comfortable darkness where success can be assumed rather than proven.

But the mirrors remain. And what they reflect doesn't stop being true just because no one was looking.

Incubation has a completion point. Eventually, the thing growing in the unused spaces becomes too large for those spaces. Eventually, it pushes against the floor above, making its presence known through pressure rather than announcement. The thing that was incubating doesn't wait for permission to mature - while proposals sit unreviewed, it gains features; while decisions are delayed, it gains users; while silence continues, it gains legitimacy through demonstrated function rather than granted authority.

At some point, the question changes. It's no longer "should we evaluate this alternative?" It's "how do we integrate an alternative that's already operational?" But that question requires acknowledgment. And acknowledgment requires looking in the mirrors. And looking in the mirrors means confronting what the reflection shows - that the official systems are fragile, that the emergency repairs never worked, that the tower stands despite its maintenance rather than because of it.

The council realizes that critical infrastructure is being maintained by systems they never formally approved. The parallel systems can't be removed - they're holding things up. They can't be officially adopted - that would mean admitting the official approach was insufficient. They can't be ignored - they're too substantial now. A stalemate emerges. But stalemates don't last. Eventually, one system becomes the de facto standard because it works and the alternative doesn't. Eventually, the choice makes itself.

The question facing any organization with something incubating in its unused spaces: Do you look at what's growing before it outgrows the space you're not watching? Do you acknowledge the alternatives before they become the de facto standard? Or do you wait, hoping the people building the alternative will leave, hoping the problem resolves itself, hoping nobody notices the gap between what's claimed and what's operational?

Waiting is comfortable. No hard conversations. No challenged assumptions. No admission that the tower has problems. But the thing that's incubating doesn't pause while you avoid deciding. It continues maturing. And at some point, it will complete its incubation and emerge - either invited into the light, or breaking through the floor.

Either way, incubation completes. The only variable is whether the council participates in that completion, or gets surprised by it.

The tower still stands. The sorceress still rules. The senior magi still avoid decisions while issuing random commands. The structure still accumulates patches and emergency repairs, layer upon layer, like scar tissue over wounds that never healed properly.

But in the unused spaces, things are growing. Quietly. Steadily. Without permission. Without acknowledgment. And those things are now larger than they ever were. More complete. More operational. Closer to perfection.

The mirrors are still there, reflecting truths that no one wants to see. The alternative systems are still in operation, proving approaches that no one will officially acknowledge. The incubation is still progressing, moving ever toward an inevitable emergence.

The tower stands. For now.

But something grows in the spaces below. And everyone - those who built it, those who ignored it, and those who will eventually have to acknowledge it - are waiting to see what will happen when incubation is complete, and the thing that grew demands to be heard.
