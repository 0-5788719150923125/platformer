# Head-to-Head

*A decision matrix for people who weren't given directions.*

---

Every organization solves the same problems. The differences are in which tradeoffs they chose-and whether they chose them consciously at all.

This document compares approaches **head-to-head**: the pattern this organization adopted versus an alternative that addresses the same problem differently. In some cases the alternative is industry standard. In others it's simply *coherent*. The point isn't that one column is always right. The point is that if you can't articulate why you chose what you chose, you didn't choose-you defaulted.

---

## The Matrix

| Problem Domain | Approach A (Adopted) | Approach B (Alternative) | Tradeoffs |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Deployment Topology** | **Hand-to-hand**. Every deployment depends on a dozen teams handing off artifacts, tickets, and permissions sequentially. Deployment is a chain of custody. | **End-to-End**. Many teams, one codebase, one pipeline. The team that writes the code deploys the code. No handoffs, no relay batons. | <ins>A optimizes for</ins> distributed accountability (no single point of blame).<br><br><ins>B optimizes for</ins> velocity and ownership.<br><br><ins>A sacrifices</ins> speed - a 4-hour deploy becomes a 4-week deploy because 11 teams each need 2 business days.<br><br><ins>B sacrifices</ins> distributed blame - if it breaks, it was your fault. |
| **Scale Response** | **Linear Scaling** (add people). Hundreds of accounts require proportionally more operators. Complexity managed by duplication. Each new account is a new directory, a new pipeline, a new thing to maintain. | **Logarithmic Scaling** (add patterns). Hundreds of accounts managed by pattern matching. New accounts automatically targeted. Complexity managed by abstraction. Adding accounts adds zero operational burden. | <ins>A optimizes for</ins> employment - more accounts = more jobs.<br><br><ins>B optimizes for</ins> efficiency - more accounts = same team.<br><br><ins>A sacrifices</ins> efficiency - linear scaling is a losing strategy against exponential infrastructure growth.<br><br><ins>B sacrifices</ins> employment - logarithmic scaling means the same team manages 10x the accounts. |
| **Velocity** | **Breadth over Depth** (staff the gap). When delivery is slow, add headcount. Distribute work across more people rather than accelerating the work itself. Velocity is measured in team size and sprint points, not in outcomes per engineer. The approach works - things do get delivered - but the ratio of effort to output climbs steadily, and the coordination overhead of larger teams becomes its own workstream. | **Depth over Breadth** (tool the gap). When delivery is slow, build leverage. Invest in tooling and architecture that lets a small team produce disproportionate output. A new capability goes from concept to production in days because the framework was designed for extension. The team stays small because the marginal cost of the next deployment approaches zero. | <ins>A optimizes for</ins> organizational legibility - headcount is visible, leverage is not; a larger team is easier to justify in a budget review.<br><br><ins>B optimizes for</ins> output per engineer - fewer people, better tooling, higher individual impact.<br><br><ins>A sacrifices</ins> efficiency - coordination cost grows with team size; at some point, a meaningful share of the team's energy is spent on the overhead of being a team.<br><br><ins>B sacrifices</ins> redundancy - high-leverage teams are fragile to attrition; when one person represents disproportionate output, losing them is an disproportionate loss. |
| **Development Philosophy** | **Task-Driven** (path of least resistance). Work is defined by tickets. Each ticket is a discrete unit - scoped, estimated, assigned, completed, closed. Architecture emerges accidentally from the accumulated decisions of hundreds of tickets, none of which were responsible for the whole. The system's shape is the sum of its patches. This works - things get built, tickets get closed, sprints get completed. But the resulting architecture is whatever fell out of the backlog, not something anyone designed. When problems arise, they're addressed with more tickets. The mental model is reactive: problems exist to be solved, and solving them means closing tickets. | **Architecture-Driven** (preventative design). Work is defined by the system's target state. Individual tasks exist, but they serve a coherent architectural vision rather than defining one. Problems are anticipated and addressed structurally before they manifest as incidents. The framework is designed so that entire categories of failure become impossible, not just individually patched. This looks like premature optimization to people who measure progress in tickets closed - but it's only premature if the problems aren't real. Four-week deploy cycles, non-functional patch management, and 1am maintenance windows are not hypothetical future problems. They are current ones. The question isn't whether to optimize; it's whether to keep treating symptoms or address the cause. | <ins>A optimizes for</ins> predictability - every unit of work has a ticket, an estimate, and a definition of done; progress is visible and measurable in sprint reviews.<br><br><ins>B optimizes for</ins> leverage - investing in architecture today eliminates entire categories of tickets tomorrow; progress is measured in problems that stop recurring.<br><br><ins>A sacrifices</ins> coherence - a system shaped by tickets has no architect; each fix is locally rational but globally arbitrary; after enough patches, the system's complexity becomes its own primary source of new tickets.<br><br><ins>B sacrifices</ins> predictability - architectural work is harder to estimate, harder to decompose into sprint-sized chunks, and harder to justify to stakeholders who equate visible motion with progress. |
| **Authority Model** | **Hierarchical** (org-chart-driven). VPs evaluate architecture they cannot implement. Directors approve designs they cannot read. Authority flows from org chart, not capability. | **Capability-Based** (meritocratic). Architecture evaluated by people who can implement alternatives. Designs approved by people who can identify failure modes. Authority earned by demonstrated competence. | <ins>A optimizes for</ins> organizational structure - clear chain of command.<br><br><ins>B optimizes for</ins> decision quality - evaluators can distinguish good from bad.<br><br><ins>A sacrifices</ins> decision quality - "notable by its absence" is the response to order-of-magnitude performance improvements.<br><br><ins>B sacrifices</ins> organizational structure - meritocracy is uncomfortable for people whose authority derives from tenure. |
| **Specialization** | **Siloed** (fragmented). Engineers deeply specialized in fragments. One person knows VPCs. Another knows IAM. No one understands the system. Specialization prevents improvement because no one can see the whole. | **End-to-End** (full-stack ownership). Engineers own entire service stacks. The person who writes the compute configuration also writes the networking, storage, and deployment pipeline. | <ins>A optimizes for</ins> depth of expertise in narrow domains.<br><br><ins>B optimizes for</ins> breadth of understanding - engineers who see the whole can improve the whole.<br><br><ins>A sacrifices</ins> system-level thinking - specialists optimize locally, pessimize globally.<br><br><ins>B sacrifices</ins> depth - generalists may miss edge cases that specialists catch. |
| **Process vs Outcome** | **Ceremonial** (Scrum theater). Sprint velocity tracked. Burndown charts maintained. Retrospectives conducted. "Done" celebrated. Whether the deployed infrastructure actually works is not measured. | **Evidence-Based** (measured). Success measured by: Can you destroy and recreate this in one hour? Do patches actually apply? Do passwords actually rotate? Does the dashboard reflect reality? | <ins>A optimizes for</ins> process compliance - the team followed the process.<br><br><ins>B optimizes for</ins> results - the infrastructure works.<br><br><ins>A sacrifices</ins> results - velocity measures motion, not progress.<br><br><ins>B sacrifices</ins> process compliance - outcome-focused teams may skip ceremonies that provide alignment. |
| **Best Practices** | **Invoked** (as a shield). "We followed best practices" is the preamble to every architecture discussion, every design review, every pushback against change. The phrase is never followed by a citation. It doesn't need to be-"best practices" isn't a technical claim, it's a conversation-ender. It means "we did what everyone else does" and therefore cannot be questioned. The practices themselves are never enumerated, never evaluated against the specific problem, never revisited as constraints change. They are inherited, not chosen. | **Understood and Selectively Rejected** (as engineering). Best practices are read, understood, and then evaluated against the actual problem. In many cases Platformer deliberately violates them-local modules instead of versioned remote references, deep-merged YAML instead of HCL variables, a single state per account-region instead of granular state isolation-because the best practice optimizes for the lowest common denominator, not for the cohesive ecosystem. Every deviation is a conscious decision with a reason that can be articulated on demand. | <ins>A optimizes for</ins> defensibility - no one gets fired for following best practices.<br><br><ins>B optimizes for</ins> fitness - the solution matches the problem, not the industry default.<br><br><ins>A sacrifices</ins> fitness - best practices for 5 accounts are anti-patterns at 500; "everyone does it this way" is not an argument when everyone has the same problems.<br><br><ins>B sacrifices</ins> defensibility - deviating from convention means owning the outcome; if it breaks, you can't hide behind the crowd. |
| **Onboarding** | **Sink-or-Swim**. Dozens of decision points, multiple failure modes, half a dozen repositories, days per deployment. No documentation. New employees in permanent onboarding. Those who survive become defenders of the complexity that nearly drowned them. | **Guided Descent**. State fragments as building blocks. Tests as documentation. Schema as reference. New operators compose fragments and run `terraform plan` within hours, not weeks. | <ins>A optimizes for</ins> natural selection - survivors deeply understand the system (or at least their corner of it).<br><br><ins>B optimizes for</ins> accessibility - more people can contribute sooner.<br><br><ins>A sacrifices</ins> contribution velocity - most new engineers leave within 6 months, taking industry perspectives with them.<br><br><ins>B sacrifices</ins> depth of suffering - engineers who didn't nearly drown may underestimate the currents. |
| **Self-Service Model** | **Altruistic Building**. Dozens of Port.io actions built for imagined consumers. Zero adoption. The builders don't use their own tools. Self-service for others who didn't ask. | **Pragmatic Building**. Build what you need. Use it daily. Refine through use. Then share when it's battle-tested. Self-service starts with the self. | <ins>A optimizes for</ins> organizational optics - "we built self-service!" sounds good in a slide deck.<br><br><ins>B optimizes for</ins> actual utility - tools refined by daily use are usable by others.<br><br><ins>A sacrifices</ins> adoption - tools built for imaginary users serve imaginary needs.<br><br><ins>B sacrifices</ins> optics - "I built this for myself" sounds less impressive than "I built a platform." |
| **Environment Parity** | **Impossible by Design** (structural divergence). Each environment is a different directory with different code. Dev, staging, and production diverge the moment they're created - different module versions, different variable defaults, different manual modifications accumulated over months. Parity isn't a gap to be closed; it's a property the architecture cannot express. The directory *is* the environment, so different directories are different environments by definition. "Works in dev" and "works in prod" are statements about two unrelated codebases that happen to share a name. | **Impossible to Violate** (structural identity). Every environment runs the same code. The only difference is which state fragments load and which `var.aws_region` targets. Dev and prod diverge in configuration data, never in logic. Parity isn't maintained through discipline - it's enforced by the architecture. There is no mechanism to give one account different code than another, because there is only one codebase. | <ins>A optimizes for</ins> per-environment flexibility - each directory can be customized without affecting others.<br><br><ins>B optimizes for</ins> confidence - what you tested is what you deploy, byte-for-byte.<br><br><ins>A sacrifices</ins> confidence - the gap between environments is unknowable because no tool can diff two directories that were never meant to be identical.<br><br><ins>B sacrifices</ins> per-environment flexibility - environments that genuinely need different logic require the framework to express that difference as data, not as code forks. |
| **Configuration Management** | **Lateral Duplication**. Tens of thousands of Terraform files, each a copy-pasted sibling. Changes propagate laterally by manual replication across directories. | **Hierarchical Composition**. One codebase, state fragments merged in precedence order. Configuration flows downward from policy through layers of specificity. | <ins>A optimizes for</ins> isolation - each deployment is independent (and independently broken).<br><br><ins>B optimizes for</ins> consistency - every deployment converges toward the same specification.<br><br><ins>A sacrifices</ins> consistency - drift between siblings is invisible until it isn't.<br><br><ins>B sacrifices</ins> isolation - a bad merge affects everything it targets. |
| **Module Coupling** | **Direct Coupling**. Module A imports Module B's outputs. They know each other by name. Changing B's interface breaks A. | **Dependency Inversion**. Modules face outward, emitting requests. An orchestrator collects and connects. Modules never reference each other. | <ins>A optimizes for</ins> simplicity of initial wiring.<br><br><ins>B optimizes for</ins> testability, replaceability, and independent evolution.<br><br><ins>A sacrifices</ins> long-term maintainability - every new module tightens the mesh.<br><br><ins>B sacrifices</ins> immediate readability - the indirection requires understanding the pattern. |
| **Abstractions** | **Type-per-Module** (tree-level). Separate module for EC2, separate for EKS, separate for ECS. Separate pipeline for Nessus, separate for CrowdStrike, separate for password rotation. Different interfaces, different patterns, different mental models - at every altitude. Each compute type is its own module with its own API. Each operational task is its own engineering project with its own repository, credentials, and inventory. The organization works tree by tree: one module per resource type, one pipeline per task, one bespoke solution per problem. No one looks up to see the forest because the trees are all they've ever tended. Every tree gets its own gardener, its own watering schedule, its own soil analysis - even when the underlying operation is identical: put a file on a machine and run it. | **Type-as-Routing** (forest-level). One compute interface. `type: ec2` routes to EC2 handler. `type: eks` routes to EKS handler. Same configuration shape, different implementations. The same principle extends to every altitude: Platformer doesn't have a Nessus module, a CrowdStrike module, and a password rotation module. It has generic primitives - secret replication, SSM document execution, scheduled automation, Helm chart deployment - that compose into any operational task. Installing CrowdStrike is a state fragment that composes these primitives: replicate the installer secret, execute the SSM document, schedule the reconciliation. Installing Nessus would be the same primitives with different parameters. The framework sees the forest: it doesn't know or care what it's installing. It knows how to put things on machines, how to keep them there, and how to verify they're running. The specifics are data, not code. | <ins>A optimizes for</ins> clarity - each module does one thing, and its name tells you what; there is no abstraction to learn, no primitives to compose, no framework to understand.<br><br><ins>B optimizes for</ins> consistency and leverage - consumers don't care about implementation; every new task is a configuration change, not an engineering project; the twentieth agent install is ten lines of YAML, not a new repository.<br><br><ins>A sacrifices</ins> consistency and leverage - every compute type has a different API; migration between types requires rewriting configuration; the organization builds the same pipeline over and over, each time from scratch, each time slightly different, each time maintained independently; the hundredth bespoke pipeline costs as much as the first because nothing was learned, nothing was reused, and nothing compounds.<br><br><ins>B sacrifices</ins> transparency and clarity of purpose - the routing layer hides implementation details that sometimes matter; a generic "put things on machines" primitive is harder to explain in a design review than "the Nessus pipeline"; abstraction trades immediate legibility for long-term compounding. |
| **Version Pinning** | **Pin-and-Pray**. Thousands of module references tracking `ref=master` (changes silently on every push). Thousands more pinned across dozens of unique version tags (never updated). Both strategies fail differently. | **Ship-the-Source**. Modules live in the same repository. No version pins because there are no remote references. The code you test is the code you deploy, byte-for-byte. | <ins>A optimizes for</ins> reusability across repositories (in theory).<br><br><ins>B optimizes for</ins> integrity - no version skew, no phantom updates, no stale pins.<br><br><ins>A sacrifices</ins> integrity - `ref=master` means your infrastructure changes when someone else pushes; pinned versions mean you never get security fixes.<br><br><ins>B sacrifices</ins> reusability - modules tightly coupled to the repository they live in. |
| **Code Generation** | **Fire-and-Forget** (the deployment generator). An Ansible/Jinja2 template is run once to generate HCL. The generated code is committed to a repository and maintained by hand forever after. The generator is never run again. The template and the output diverge immediately. Every tenant environment is a snowflake within days of generation because operators modify the output directly. There are now hundreds of these snowflakes, each a handcrafted artifact that no one can reproduce. | **Living Template** (compositional). There is no generator because there is no generated code. Configuration is declared in state fragments. The same Terraform code runs for every tenant. Differences between tenants are data, not code. If you need to change the pattern, you change it once-in code-and every tenant converges on next apply. | <ins>A optimizes for</ins> speed of initial deployment - generating a tenant directory is fast the first time.<br><br><ins>B optimizes for</ins> maintainability - the hundredth tenant is as easy as the first.<br><br><ins>A sacrifices</ins> maintainability - the generator produces a child it immediately abandons; maintenance falls to whoever touches the output next; after hundreds of tenants, each hand-modified, no one can tell what was generated and what was grafted on.<br><br><ins>B sacrifices</ins> speed of initial deployment - composing state fragments requires understanding the fragment system; there is no "just generate it and move on." |
| **Git Usage** | **Git-as-Database**. Frontend (Port.io) writes Terraform HCL directly to git. Git becomes a transactional store. Configuration files treated as data structures to be programmatically modified. | **Git-as-Ledger**. Git stores code. Data lives in a proper datastore (CMDB, Parameter Store, Secrets Manager). Git changes are human-authored or human-reviewed. | <ins>A optimizes for</ins> simplicity - one store for everything.<br><br><ins>B optimizes for</ins> separation of concerns - code changes require review; data changes don't.<br><br><ins>A sacrifices</ins> integrity - programmatically-generated HCL is fragile and hard to review.<br><br><ins>B sacrifices</ins> simplicity - multiple data stores means multiple integration points. |
| **Change Propagation** | **One-to-One** (manual per-account). A change to patch baselines means opening hundreds of deployment directories and editing each file. One change, one directory, one at a time. | **One-to-Many** (pattern-targeted). A change to a state fragment propagates to every account matching a pattern. One change, many deployments, zero manual replication. | <ins>A optimizes for</ins> control - you see every file you touch.<br><br><ins>B optimizes for</ins> leverage - you describe intent once; the system fulfills it everywhere.<br><br><ins>A sacrifices</ins> leverage - a 2-line change becomes a 4-week project.<br><br><ins>B sacrifices</ins> granular control - you must trust the pattern matcher. |
| **Deployment Lifecycle** | **Cradle-to-Grave** (pets). Each environment deployed once, manually modified over months/years, unique and irreplaceable. Destroying it is unthinkable-no one knows if it can be recreated. | **Disposable** (cattle). Environments are disposable. Destroy and recreate is a test, not a disaster. The deployment process is permanent; the infrastructure is ephemeral. | <ins>A optimizes for</ins> stability of long-running environments.<br><br><ins>B optimizes for</ins> reproducibility - if you can't destroy it, you don't own it.<br><br><ins>A sacrifices</ins> reproducibility - after 6 months of manual changes, the code no longer describes the environment.<br><br><ins>B sacrifices</ins> accumulated state - long-running stateful services require migration strategies. |
| **Disaster Recovery** | **Snapshot-Based** (Veeam-managed). Disaster recovery is delegated to IT, who manage a team of SREs each responsible for Veeam backup configurations across fleets of servers. EC2 instances and RDS databases run scheduled snapshots. Recovery means restoring the most recent snapshot and hoping the gap between the snapshot and the failure is small enough to survive. The backup strategy is bolted onto the infrastructure after the fact - a separate system, maintained by a separate team, with its own schedules, retention policies, and failure modes that do not communicate with the infrastructure lifecycle. The backup knows nothing about what it is backing up. The infrastructure knows nothing about its backups. Recovery is a point-in-time jump to a prior state with no guarantee that the restored state is internally consistent across services that were snapshotted at different times. | **Sequence-Modeled** (lifecycle-reversible). Disaster recovery is structural, not bolted on. Because infrastructure is declared in state fragments and every lifecycle event is an additive operation in `top.yaml`, recovery is traversal of a convergence graph - forward to deploy, backward to revert. The system models lifecycle tasks as a state machine: each transition is a node, and the graph can be walked in either direction. Data is treated as the durable layer that outlives ephemeral infrastructure cycles - databases, volumes, and object stores are backed up as part of the declaration, not as an afterthought. Infrastructure is rebuilt around the data rather than restored alongside it. The backup is not a snapshot of the machine; it is the data the machine was built to serve, plus the declaration that rebuilds the machine from nothing. | <ins>A optimizes for</ins> familiarity - Veeam is a known quantity with vendor support, documented procedures, and an SRE team trained on its operations; recovery is a button press in a console the team already knows.<br><br><ins>B optimizes for</ins> structural integrity - recovery is a property of the system's design, not a bolt-on managed by a separate team; the infrastructure and its recovery procedure are the same artifact.<br><br><ins>A sacrifices</ins> consistency - snapshots taken at different times across different services produce a restored state where the database is from 2:00 AM, the application server is from 1:45 AM, and the configuration is from last Tuesday; cross-service consistency is not guaranteed and is rarely tested.<br><br><ins>B sacrifices</ins> tooling maturity - Veeam has been doing this for decades; a sequence-modeled approach built into an infrastructure framework is novel and unproven at the scale of a real multi-service, multi-database disaster scenario; the theory is sound but the recovery has not been tested under the conditions that matter most: total loss, time pressure, and executive panic. |
| **Praxis** | **Witness Protection** ([zero-knowledge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zero-knowledge_proof)). The witness is the one thing in a zero-knowledge proof that must never be compromised. In cryptography, it is the secret that makes the proof valid. In engineering, it is the person who verified the system works - who stood at the mouth of the cave, called random paths, and watched the correct response every time. We protect the witness through people and process. Code review exists because no single engineer should be the sole validator of production changes. Peer review, change management, and approval chains exist because verification must be distributed - no one person's conviction should be sufficient to ship. On-call rotations exist because the system must be operable at 3am by someone who did not build it. Documentation exists because knowledge must survive personnel changes. The discipline is organizational: redundancy of knowledge, separation of duties, institutional memory that outlives any individual contributor. The witness is protected by ensuring they are never the only one who knows. If one person leaves, another can step in - not because they memorized the system, but because the process ensured the knowledge was never concentrated in the first place. The rigor is not aesthetic. It is protective. Rigorous process protects the people who depend on the system: the operators who trust the runbook at 3am, the engineers who inherit the team after someone leaves, the tenants whose data lives in infrastructure they will never see. The proof is transferable because it lives in process, not in any individual's head. | **Witness Protection** ([zero-knowledge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zero-knowledge_proof)). The witness is the one thing in a zero-knowledge proof that must never be compromised. In cryptography, it is the secret that makes the proof valid. But the zero-knowledge property does not protect the witness by distributing it, or by guarding it, or by writing it down. It protects the witness by designing a protocol where verification never requires its disclosure. The Verifier gains conviction. The witness is never exposed. That is protection by construction, not by policy. In engineering, the witness is the person who built the system and knows where the truth lives. The organization needs their conviction but not their testimony. The protocol that protects them is a system that can be verified without them - not a runbook that documents what they know, but a codebase that proves what they proved, to anyone who runs it, without requiring the author to explain, to be present, or to be called at 3am because they are the only one who understands. You do not protect a witness by posting guards. You protect a witness by building a world where no one ever needs to ask them to testify. | There are no tradeoffs in this row. This is the one entry where both approaches arrive at the same conclusion through different paths and find themselves standing in the same place. The witness must be protected. The proof must be sound. The discipline must be real. The people must be safe. Everything else in this document is a disagreement about method. This is an agreement about purpose. |
| **Migration Strategy** | **Step-by-Step Surgery** (the 1am rodeo). Four PRs, staged during business hours, merged sequentially at 1am by hand. Apply #1 bumps a module version and renames keys. Apply #2 comments out addon blocks to delete them, moves resources between locals, adds a new module, and runs manual `terraform state mv` commands. Apply #3 uncomments the code that Apply #2 commented out. Apply #4 removes a temporary flag that Apply #2 added. Known issues include bricking nodes if permissions are removed in the wrong order, addons failing to recreate, and pod identity associations that must be manually deleted via the AWS CLI or console. Five hours. For a version bump to a cluster that was working fine. | **Atomic Convergence** (declare and apply). The desired end state is declared in configuration. Terraform computes the diff. One apply. If the module's interface changed, the code is updated to match the new interface-once. No commenting and uncommenting. No state surgery. No 1am maintenance windows. If the migration is too risky for a single apply, the answer is "don't migrate"-because the old version was working. | <ins>A optimizes for</ins> risk distribution - each step is small and theoretically reversible.<br><br><ins>B optimizes for</ins> simplicity - one diff, one review, one apply; the infrastructure either converges or it doesn't.<br><br><ins>A sacrifices</ins> simplicity - four PRs means four points of failure, four reviews, four chances for human error at 1am; the "Known Issues" section is longer than the procedure itself.<br><br><ins>B sacrifices</ins> granular control - you can't pause mid-apply to run CLI commands and fix things by hand. (That's a feature, not a bug.) |
| **Blast Radius** | **Wall-to-Wall** (everything). One repository, one branch, one commit: every account affected simultaneously. No canary. No gradual rollout. The blast radius is the entire fleet. | **Ring-by-Ring** (graduated). Canary accounts first. Then dev. Then staging. Then production, in waves. Bad commits caught early, blast radius contained. | <ins>A optimizes for</ins> deployment simplicity - one apply, everything updated.<br><br><ins>B optimizes for</ins> safety - bad changes affect few accounts before detection.<br><br><ins>A sacrifices</ins> safety - a misconfigured `for_each` destroys everything simultaneously.<br><br><ins>B sacrifices</ins> speed - graduated rollout is slower than fleet-wide apply. |
| **Task Automation** | **Pipeline-per-Task** (bespoke). Every operational task gets its own dedicated GitHub Actions workflow, its own repository, its own inventory, its own credentials setup. Installing CrowdStrike? New workflow. Rotating a certificate? New workflow. Each trivial task spawns a parallel automation universe with its own lifecycle, its own drift, its own maintainer. Nothing is tracked in state. Nothing converges. | **State-Tracked** (convergent). Operational tasks are state fragments. Installing CrowdStrike is a YAML block referencing a secrets replication module and an SSM document. The task is declared, tracked in Terraform state, and reconciled on every apply. Adding a new agent to every instance is a 10-line fragment, not a new repository. | <ins>A optimizes for</ins> autonomy - each team owns their pipeline; no coordination required.<br><br><ins>B optimizes for</ins> convergence - every task is part of the same state; drift detected; removal is as clean as addition.<br><br><ins>A sacrifices</ins> convergence - pipelines don't know about each other; the CrowdStrike workflow doesn't know the instance was terminated by the patching workflow; state is invisible, so conflicts are invisible.<br><br><ins>B sacrifices</ins> autonomy - teams must contribute to a shared codebase instead of spinning up their own. |
| **Ownership Model** | **Minimum Viable Effort** (hot potato). Work begins at task assignment and ends at the earliest defensible handoff. The goal is not to solve the problem - it's to make the problem someone else's problem. Engineers do the minimum required to justify passing the ticket to the next person in the chain. Ownership is a liability to be shed, not a responsibility to be held. The result is a system where every component was built by someone trying to leave as quickly as possible, and it shows: nothing is finished, nothing is polished, nothing works beyond the narrow scope of the original ticket. When something breaks, the first question is "whose is this?" - not to fix it, but to establish that it isn't yours. | **Extreme Ownership** (sovereign). One person decides the problem is theirs and solves it end-to-end. No assignment necessary. No permission requested. No handoff planned. Platformer exists because someone said "f*** it, I'll do this myself" - and then did. Every module, every state fragment, every test, every document was written by a team that chose to own the outcome rather than deflect it. The work isn't partitioned into sprint-sized tickets designed to be individually disclaimed. It's a cohesive system built by people who refused to let "that's not my job" be the epitaph of their engineering career. | <ins>A optimizes for</ins> individual risk minimization - if you never own anything, nothing is ever your fault.<br><br><ins>B optimizes for</ins> outcomes - if you own everything, the system actually works.<br><br><ins>A sacrifices</ins> outcomes - a system built by people fleeing responsibility has no architect, no vision, and no one who cares whether it works after the ticket is closed; every component reflects exactly the amount of effort required to make it Someone Else's Problem.<br><br><ins>B sacrifices</ins> risk distribution - unconditional ownership means unconditional accountability; when it breaks, there's no chain of handoffs to hide behind, and no ambiguity about who should fix it. |
| **Risk Perception** | **Change-as-Threat** (defensive). The primary risk is doing something wrong, so the safest move is doing as little as possible. Changes require maintenance windows, staged rollouts across four PRs, and 1am merges - not because the change is dangerous, but because *all change* is presumed dangerous. Meanwhile, the actual risks - non-functional patch management, unrotated passwords, drifted infrastructure - accumulate silently for months because they don't involve changing anything. Stagnation is invisible. The threat model protects against action and ignores inaction. | **Stagnation-as-Threat** (offensive). The primary risk is *not* changing. Infrastructure that isn't continuously reconciled drifts. Modules that aren't updated accumulate tech debt. Patterns that aren't challenged calcify into assumptions. Platformer treats every `terraform apply` as both a deployment and a health check. The system assumes it will change constantly and is designed for safe, frequent mutation - not careful, infrequent surgery. The dangerous state isn't "we changed something." ## Reading the Matrix

A few observations emerge from the aggregate:

**Approach A optimizes for the present moment.** Each decision makes sense in isolation, at the time it was made, for the team that made it. Copy-paste is faster than abstraction when you have 3 accounts. Manual naming works when one person remembers everything. Hardcoded secrets are fine when the repository is private and the team is small.

**Approach B optimizes for the next order of magnitude.** Patterns that work at 5 accounts, 50 accounts, 500 accounts-without changing the approach. The upfront cost is higher. The marginal cost approaches zero.

**The transition between A and B is the hardest part.** Not because B is technically difficult, but because A is *familiar*. The people who survived A have built expertise around its quirks. Switching to B devalues that expertise. This isn't hypothetical-it's the mechanism described in `sunk-costs.md`, `unearned-authority.md`, and `survivorship-bias.md` (if it existed; it's implied everywhere).

**Neither column is universally correct.** Approach B has real costs: abstraction hides detail, pattern matching can mis-target, unified codebases have fleet-wide blast radius. The question isn't which column is right. The question is: *did you choose, or did you default?*

## The Meta-Pattern

Every row in this matrix follows the same arc:

1. **A reasonable decision** is made under constraints (time, knowledge, team size, account count).
2. **The constraints change** (more accounts, more tenants, more complexity, more compliance requirements).
3. **The decision doesn't change** because changing it would require acknowledging that the original decision, while reasonable, is no longer optimal.
4. **The cost compounds** silently until someone notices that a 2-line configuration change takes 4 weeks, or that patch management has been non-functional for a year, or that dozens of self-service actions have zero users.

The matrix isn't a judgment of the people who made these decisions. It's a map of where the organization is and where the roads lead from here.

Every row is a fork. Some forks were taken deliberately. Most were taken by inertia.

The document is called *Head-to-Head* because that's what honest comparison requires: putting two approaches next to each other, and flipping a coin.

Our customers are our peers.
